Section 01

The CI Specialist Role

Purpose, daily operating cadence and core responsibilities on site

Purpose

The Continuous Improvement Specialist is responsible for driving structured, data-led improvement across site operations. The role sits between the floor and leadership — translating operational problems into evidence-based projects, delivering measurable results, and building a culture where improvement is a daily habit rather than a reactive fix.

The CI Specialist does not just manage projects. They observe, question, measure, and challenge — constantly looking for the gap between what is happening and what should be happening, then leading the effort to close that gap permanently.

Core mandate: Identify waste. Quantify the problem. Deliver a sustainable fix. Prove it worked.

Core Responsibilities

Operational

  • Conduct structured floor observations across all operational areas
  • Log waste, defects and process deviations as they are observed
  • Track and analyse site KPIs against targets daily
  • Identify and escalate performance signals before they become problems
  • Lead and facilitate problem-solving sessions with Team Leaders and associates

Project & Programme

  • Manage a portfolio of active improvement projects at varying levels of complexity
  • Write project charters, define scope and set measurable targets
  • Apply the correct methodology (DMAIC, Quick Win, Investigation) to each project type
  • Write SOPs for all improved processes and brief operational teams
  • Close projects with verified, sustained results and hand over to operations

Section 02

Observation Workflow

From floor walk to structured log — turning what you see into data

Why Observations Matter

Observations are the raw material of CI. Without a disciplined habit of logging what is seen on the floor, opportunities pass unrecorded and problems recur. The observation workflow converts informal walkthroughs into structured, searchable data — creating an evidence base that drives the idea pipeline and supports root cause analysis.

On a fulfillment operation, the floor walk covers all active process areas: inbound dock, stow buffer, pick floor, pack stations and despatch. Each area carries distinct waste patterns. Inbound tends to surface inventory and waiting waste; pick surfaces motion and defects; pack surfaces overprocessing and defects; despatch surfaces transport and overproduction. Structured observation across all lanes, every day, builds the evidence base the programme depends on.

The Eight Wastes (TIMWOODS)

During each floor walk, the CI Specialist actively looks for the eight categories of waste. Every observation is logged against one of these types in Continuum, which builds a searchable, filterable record over time.

Waste TypeWhat to Look For on SiteCommon Signals
TransportUnnecessary movement of product, cages or materials further than needed between pick locations, buffer, pack stations or dockHLOP travel between pick locations and cage buffer; cages moving to buffer then back; consumables not positioned at pack stations
InventoryExcess cages, product or consumables held beyond immediate need: cages stacking in buffer, inbound pallets waiting on dock, stow buffer backlogPick cages queuing beyond buffer capacity; inbound product on dock waiting IDRT check; stow buffer backlog delaying putaway
MotionUnnecessary movement by associates or HLOP travelling to charging areas, Problem Solve or between zones rather than completing work at point of needHLOP driving to charging area mid-pick cycle; associates walking to PS/ICQA to report exceptions; awkward bin positions causing repeated reaching
WaitingIdle time for associates, HLOPs or processes waiting for scanner response, cage availability, system decision or batch releaseScanner login sequence (YubiKey + PIN); idle time between batch releases; queue at SLAM machine; waiting at buffer handoff for cage scan
OverproductionStages running ahead of downstream capacity, with pick outpacing pack or pack outpacing despatch and volume building before the next stage is readyPick floor cages accumulating beyond buffer capacity; outbound sorted before load team is ready; pack building up ahead of SLAM availability

Structuring an Observation Log Entry

Each observation logged in Continuum captures the following information to ensure it is actionable and traceable:

Date & Shift
When was it observed? Day/night, shift time
Area / Zone
Where exactly? Zone, bay, department, workstation
Waste Type
Which of the eight wastes does this belong to?
Description
What was seen? Specific, factual, no assumptions
Estimated Impact
Units per hour lost, time lost, people affected, frequency
Linked Idea
Raise as an idea in the pipeline if actionable
Discipline point: Observations must be logged on the day they are made. A mental note becomes worthless overnight. The value is in the searchable, dated record — not the memory.

From Observation to Idea

Not every observation immediately becomes an idea. Some are single data points — part of a pattern that only becomes visible when several observations are reviewed together. The CI Specialist reviews the observation log weekly, looking for patterns by waste type, area or shift, and raises ideas where a cluster of related observations suggests a structured improvement is warranted.

Single observation — logged, tagged, dated. Filed for review.
↓
Pattern identified — 3+ related observations across area or time window. Review triggered.
↓
Idea raised — problem statement drafted, metric identified, linked to portfolio. Enters idea pipeline.
Section 03

The Idea Pipeline

How potential improvements are captured, evaluated and converted into live projects

Pipeline Overview

The idea pipeline is the structured funnel through which all improvement opportunities pass before becoming active projects. It prevents reactive, ad-hoc project starts and ensures that work begins with a clear problem statement, a defined metric and a validated business case.

Idea Pipeline — Four Stages
Stage 1IDEA
Stage 2DEFINITION
Stage 3VALIDATION
Stage 4PROJECT ASSIGNED

Stage 1 — Idea

An idea is a brief statement of a problem or opportunity, raised from an observation, a discussion with a Team Leader, a data signal or a near miss. At this stage the focus is capture, not analysis. Ideas must be logged quickly before they are lost.

Entry criteria: A clear problem statement in plain language. Which area does it affect? What is the symptom? Who raised it?
  • Linked to a portfolio (which operational area does this belong to?)
  • Tagged with a relevant waste type
  • Given an initial priority score: impact × effort estimate
  • No detailed analysis required at this stage — just capture and categorise

Stage 2 — Definition

Ideas that are scored sufficiently high, or that recur from multiple observations, are progressed to Definition. The CI Specialist develops the idea into a structured problem statement and identifies a measurable metric that will prove whether a fix has worked.

Outputs at Definition: Written problem statement · Identified metric (what are we measuring?) · Current baseline (what is the number now?) · Target (what should it be?) · Scope boundaries (what is in / out of scope?)
  • The problem statement must describe the gap between current state and desired state
  • The metric must be measurable, repeatable and directly linked to the problem
  • Initial sizing — is this a Quick Win, Yellow Belt, Green Belt or Black Belt level of work?

Stage 3 — Validation

Before a project is formally opened, the definition is reviewed and validated. This stage ensures the business case is sound, the scope is realistic and the right project type has been selected. Validation is a governance checkpoint — it prevents wasted effort on poorly defined work.

Validation checks: Does the problem statement match the data? Is the target realistic? Is the right methodology selected? Is there capacity to deliver this? Does it align to portfolio priorities?
  • For larger projects (Green Belt / Black Belt): business case presented to site manager
  • For Quick Wins / Yellow Belts: CI Specialist self-validates against defined criteria
  • If the idea does not pass validation it is either returned to Definition for rework or parked

Stage 4 — Project Assigned

A validated idea becomes a live project in Continuum. A project type is confirmed, a charter is created, the stage is set to Define, and the DMAIC journey begins. The idea is removed from the pipeline and tracked in the project register instead.

Section 04

Portfolio Management

Organising projects by operational area, managing the programme as a whole

What Is a Portfolio?

A portfolio is a grouping of improvement projects and pipeline ideas that relate to the same operational area or function. Portfolios allow the CI Specialist to manage the programme at two levels simultaneously: individual project execution, and programme-wide health across all areas.

On a fulfillment operation, portfolios typically align to the main process lanes: Outbound (Pick, Pack, Despatch), Inbound and Stow, Quality and ICQA, Returns Processing, and Safety and Environment. Portfolios are defined to match how the site is actually structured operationally.

Continuum shows a funnel summary for each portfolio: how many ideas are at each pipeline stage, how many projects are active and how many have been completed. This gives an instant programme health view.

Portfolio Governance

  • Each portfolio should have at least one idea progressing through the pipeline at any given time
  • No more than 4–5 active projects running simultaneously is recommended for a single CI Specialist — quality of execution matters more than volume
  • Portfolios are reviewed monthly at minimum — more frequently if an area is under performance pressure
  • Closed projects are retained in Continuum permanently — they form the CI programme's evidence base
Portfolio balance: A healthy programme does not concentrate all effort in one area. Each active portfolio should have at least one project progressing and one idea in the pipeline — spread of coverage demonstrates CI breadth, not just depth.
Section 05

Project Types & Complexity

Matching the right methodology to the size and complexity of the problem

Overview — Five Project Types

Not every problem requires a full DMAIC project. Applying the correct level of rigour to the right problem is itself a sign of CI maturity. Overcomplicating a simple issue wastes time; underplanning a complex one leads to recurrence. The five types below represent a spectrum from fast action to full six-sigma rigour.

Quick Win

Quick Win

A clearly understood problem with an obvious, low-risk fix. Can be implemented and measured within days to a few weeks.

Typical duration: 1–4 weeks · No formal charter required · Low statistical rigour
Yellow Belt

Yellow Belt

A focused, area-level improvement using basic CI tools. Data is collected and analysed. A fix is trialled and measured before implementation.

Typical duration: 4–8 weeks · Charter required · Basic data analysis
Green Belt

Green Belt

Full DMAIC methodology with structured root cause analysis, hypothesis testing and statistical validation. Delivers significant, cross-functional improvement.

Typical duration: 8–16 weeks · Full charter · Statistical tools required
Black Belt

Black Belt

Complex, high-impact, cross-functional or multi-site problem. Advanced statistical analysis, DOE and process capability studies. Usually involves multiple workstreams.

Typical duration: 3–6 months · Sponsor required · Advanced tools
Investigation

Investigation

A scoping exercise triggered by a spike, complaint or near miss. The goal is to understand what is happening and either resolve it quickly or escalate to a formal project. Investigations are time-boxed and output-driven.

Typical duration: 1–3 weeks · Brief scoping document · Outputs: root cause summary + recommendation

Process Map — Quick Win

Quick Win — Simplified Flow
01Define Problem & Fix
02Implement Solution
03Measure & Confirm
04Document & Close

Stage 01: Write a 1-line problem statement and agree the fix with the operational TL. Stage 02: Implement immediately — physical changes, schedule adjustments, process tweaks. Stage 03: Collect 1–2 weeks of post-fix data to confirm the improvement is real and sustained. Stage 04: Document the result, write a brief SOP if the fix changes a process, close in Continuum.

Section 07

KPI Tracking & Data Analysis

How performance data is captured, monitored and turned into actionable insight

The Purpose of KPI Tracking

KPI tracking in a CI programme serves two distinct purposes. The first is operational: knowing where performance stands right now relative to target. The second is analytical: building a trend history that reveals patterns, shift-point changes and signals that would be invisible from a single data point. Both matter equally.

Logging performance data into Continuum daily creates a structured time-series record for every tracked metric. This data feeds the Excel report, informs project baselines, and — over time — demonstrates the CI programme's impact through measurable before-and-after trend shifts.

RAG Status — Red / Amber / Green

Each metric is assessed against its target using a RAG (Red / Amber / Green) framework. RAG provides an at-a-glance health check that can be communicated to any stakeholder without requiring them to understand the raw numbers.

StatusThresholdMeaningExpected Response
● GREEN ≥ 100% of target On or above target — performing as required Sustain and monitor. Document in activity log.
◐ AMBER 95%–99% of target Close to target but not there — risk of decline Investigate cause, initiate corrective action. Review within the week.
○ RED < 95% of target Significantly off target — action required Immediate root cause investigation. Escalate to site manager if sustained.

Trend Analysis

A single data point tells you where you are today. A trend tells you where you are going. Continuum builds a 90-day trend view for each metric, allowing the CI Specialist to identify:

  • Improving trend — performance moving consistently toward or beyond target. Evidence of CI impact.
  • Declining trend — performance moving away from target. Requires a project or investigation to understand root cause.
  • Flat trend with occasional spikes — likely a process stability issue. SPC may be appropriate.
  • Step change improvement — a clear shift point following a project intervention. Document this — it is your strongest evidence of impact.

Statistical Process Control (SPC) Signals

SPC is used to distinguish between normal process variation (common cause) and abnormal, assignable events (special cause). When SPC signals are present, they indicate something has genuinely changed in the process — good or bad — and requires investigation.

Common SPC signals to track:
  • Single point beyond 3σ control limit
  • 8 consecutive points on same side of mean
  • 6 consecutive points trending in one direction
  • 2 of 3 consecutive points beyond 2σ
When a signal fires:
  • Do not adjust the process without investigation
  • Identify what changed at the signal point
  • Document the investigation in Continuum
  • Take assignable cause action or confirm improvement

Week-by-Week Comparison

Beyond daily logging and 90-day trends, a weekly comparison view highlights short-term performance shifts. Comparing this week's average against last week's average — and both against the target — gives the CI Specialist and site leadership a quick performance pulse without needing to interpret a full trend chart.

This week-by-week view is included automatically in the Continuum Excel report and updated on every sync.

Reporting & Stakeholder Visibility

How CI programme data reaches site leadership, managers and wider stakeholders

Reporting Philosophy

The CI Specialist generates and owns the reporting. Stakeholders should never need to chase for a CI update — the data should be available, up to date and easy to interpret without requiring a walkthrough. The reporting framework is built around two layers: the live Continuum system (for the CI Specialist's own management) and the Excel report (for stakeholder consumption).

The principle:

If it isn't written down, it didn't happen. Every project, every data point, every observation exists in Continuum. The Excel report is the clean, shareable output of that work — not a separate system, but a live reflection of it.

The Excel CI Report

The Continuum Excel report is a professionally formatted, automatically generated workbook that captures the full programme at a point in time. It is produced by syncing Continuum with a single click and can be shared with any stakeholder without requiring them to access the system.

Section 09

Continuum — End-to-End System Overview

How all workflows connect inside the platform — a single source of truth for the CI programme

What Continuum Is

Continuum is a purpose-built CI management platform designed for site-based CI Specialists. It replaces disconnected spreadsheets, notebook logs and email chains with a single, structured system that connects observations, ideas, projects, KPIs and reporting into one coherent record.

It is used exclusively by the CI Specialist — not by the wider operational team — which means data quality is controlled, the record is consistent, and the system reflects exactly what is happening with the CI programme at any given moment.

The Single Source of Truth Principle

One system. One record. Every observation, idea, project update, KPI entry and SOP reference lives in Continuum. The Excel report is a read-only output — nothing is ever entered directly into the Excel file. This ensures the data is always consistent, always traceable, and always audit-ready. The Excel file can be regenerated at any time from the Continuum data — the system is the record of truth, the spreadsheet is the communication tool.

Summary — The CI Specialist Operating Model

The workflow described in this document is not a project management system bolted onto an operations role — it is the operating model of the role itself. Observations are not optional. Data is not collected occasionally. Projects are not started without a problem statement. SOPs are not skipped because a project has gone well.

Every element of this framework is connected: observations feed the pipeline, the pipeline feeds projects, projects feed the KPI trends, and the trends feed the reporting that demonstrates programme impact to leadership. Continuum holds the record. The Excel report communicates it. The CI Specialist drives all of it.

The measure of a CI programme is not how many projects are running — it is how many problems have been permanently solved.

Projects are started to close gaps. They are not open indefinitely. They are not stalled quietly. They deliver a verified, sustained result — and then they close. That result lives in the CI Impact register, supported by the data, and it does not go away.
