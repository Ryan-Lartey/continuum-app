Overview

The CI Life Cycle at a Glance

Six stages from observation to sustained result

Every improvement on site starts with someone noticing something: a process that wastes time, a step that causes errors, a safety concern, or simply a better way of doing something. The CI programme exists to capture those observations, investigate the right ones, and turn them into permanent fixes backed by data.

The life cycle below shows how that journey works, who is involved at each stage, and what the expected output is.

Important: A project is only closed once the improvement has held for at least four weeks. A fix that lasts two weeks is not a fix. It is a temporary patch. The CI programme only counts sustained results.
Stage 1 & 2

The Observation Pipeline

How observations reach the CI Specialist and what happens to them

Two Sources of Observations

Observations come in from two places. Both are equally important.

Source 1: Team Submissions
QR Code Form

Anyone on site scans the QR code, fills in a short form on their phone, and submits. Takes under 60 seconds.

Source 2: CI Direct
Daily Floor Walk

The CI Specialist conducts structured walks of all operational areas daily, logging waste and process deviations directly.

The QR Code Form: How It Works

1
Scan the QR code

QR codes are displayed at shift brief stations, noticeboards and team leader desks. Scan with any phone camera. No app needed.

2
Fill in the short form

Name (optional), role, area, what you observed, issue type and urgency. Plain English. No CI knowledge needed.

3
Submit

The submission is sent immediately. No follow-up needed from you. The CI Specialist picks it up from there.

4
CI Specialist reviews all submissions

Every submission is read. The CI Specialist decides whether to log it as a formal observation, raise it directly as an improvement idea, or note it for monitoring. Nothing is ignored, but not every submission becomes a project.

What makes a good observation? Specific beats vague. "Associates walking to the label printer in Zone C, estimated 3-4 times per hour" is far more useful than "motion waste in Zone C." The more specific the observation, the faster the CI Specialist can act on it.

Stages 3 & 4

From Observation to Active Project

How the CI Specialist decides what to work on and what a project actually involves

Not Every Observation Becomes a Project

A single observation is a data point. The CI Specialist monitors patterns. When the same issue appears across multiple observations, shifts or areas, it signals that a structured improvement project is warranted. Low-effort fixes are implemented quickly without a full project. More complex problems get the full treatment.

Project Types: Matched to Problem Size

The CI Specialist selects the right level of rigour for the size of the problem. Overcomplicating a simple fix wastes time. Under-planning a complex one leads to recurrence.

TypeWhen It's UsedTypical Duration
Quick Win Clear problem, obvious fix, low risk. Implement, measure, confirm, done. 1-4 weeks
Yellow Belt Area-level problem needing data collection, root cause analysis and a trialled fix 4-8 weeks
Green Belt Significant cross-functional problem requiring structured DMAIC methodology and statistical validation 8-16 weeks
Investigation A spike, complaint or safety concern requiring urgent scoping before deciding next steps 1-3 weeks

DMAIC: The Project Framework

All projects above Quick Win level follow the DMAIC framework, a structured five-stage methodology that ensures the root cause is found and fixed, not just the symptom.

About the CI Excel Report

The weekly Excel report is generated directly from the Continuum CI system and contains everything you need to understand the state of the CI programme at a glance. You do not need access to the system itself. The report brings everything to you.

TabWhat It Shows
Overview High-level summary: total projects, active vs closed, pipeline count, headline KPI performance and overall CI maturity score
CI Impact Cumulative value delivered: time saved, cost avoided, defects eliminated, and a breakdown of completed project results
KPIs Each tracked metric vs its target, RAG rated (Green = on target, Amber = close, Red = off target), with a 4-week trend view
Projects Every active project: stage, owner, target, last updated and current status
Pipeline Ideas and observations in the queue, showing what is being considered for the next project cycle
Activity A log of all CI activity over the period: floor walks, observations logged, meetings and actions completed
Maturity A scored assessment of CI maturity across six dimensions, showing where the programme is strong and where to focus next
RAG Status explained: Green means the KPI is at or above target. Amber means it is within 5% of target. Red means it is more than 5% below target and needs attention. Every KPI in the report is colour coded so you can see the picture at a glance without reading every number.

What Is Expected of You

1
Encourage your team to use the QR form

The more observations that come in, the stronger the improvement pipeline. Brief your team at shift start, put QR codes up visibly, and make it clear that submissions are acted on, not ignored.

2
Support root cause investigations

When the CI Specialist is in the Analyse stage, they will need time with your team leaders and associates. Please support access to the floor and facilitate honest conversations. The quality of the root cause depends on it.

3
Back the solution during the trial

When a fix is being trialled, it needs consistent application to get valid results. If the trial is only followed some of the time, the data is worthless. Your visibility and backing makes the difference.

4
Enforce the SOP after close

The most common reason improvements fail is that the new standard is not followed once the CI project closes. When a new SOP is issued, it needs to be embedded in your daily checks and shift briefs, not filed away.

How to Escalate a Concern

If you have a concern that needs urgent CI attention (a safety spike, a sudden performance drop or a process failing on the floor), do not wait for the next report cycle. Raise it directly with the CI Specialist the same day. Investigations can be opened within 24 hours for urgent issues.

Key Principles

Fix the process, not the person. If the same mistake keeps happening with different people, the process is wrong, not the team. CI investigates the system, not the individual.
Data first, opinion second. Every decision in a CI project is driven by data. An improvement that cannot be measured cannot be confirmed. Anecdotal evidence alone is not enough.
Small observations lead to big improvements. The most impactful projects often start from a single observation by an associate. No observation is too small to submit.
A closed project is a permanent fix. Projects are not closed until the result has sustained. When a project closes, that improvement is locked in, not revisited and not undone.
